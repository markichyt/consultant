Whether you can recover compensation for a wrongful arrest caused by a police-database error turns on the specific facts and the law of your jurisdiction. In most U.S. states a claim can arise under torts such as false imprisonment or false arrest when officers detain you without probable cause, or under federal civil-rights law (42 U.S.C. § 1983) when a constitutional right is violated by someone acting under color of law. Outcomes vary with the nature of the error, whether the department reasonably relied on the bad data, applicable immunity doctrines, and your state's statutory caps and filing deadlines. Practically: gather and preserve the arrest and booking records, release paperwork, names and badge numbers, proof of time held, and any documentation of lost wages or medical and emotional harm. Then have a local attorney evaluate the merits before the statute of limitations runs — in Texas that window can be as short as two years for some claims.

Frequently asked

Can I sue the police for wrongful arrest?
Often yes — if the arrest lacked probable cause or violated your civil rights, you may have a claim. Outcomes depend on the facts and your state's law.
How much compensation can I get?
It varies by harm — time in custody, lost wages, emotional distress — and any statutory caps in your state.
What is the statute of limitations?
Deadlines differ by state and claim type and can be as short as one to two years. Government claims may require an even earlier notice.
Do I need a lawyer?
Strongly recommended — these cases involve immunity doctrines and strict deadlines a local attorney can navigate.
What evidence do I need?
Arrest and booking records, release paperwork, badge numbers, proof of time held, and any record of the database error or losses.
